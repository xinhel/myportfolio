QueuePool: a New Way to Line Up

The Math Study Center at University of Washington is a place for students to study on intro calculus classes. Tutors are there to help students with homework problems. Given that tutors are occupied most of the time, students who need help are lined up on a paper sheet to wait for their names to be called. QueuePool is the redesign that makes waiting easier and more productive.

Overview

I worked with another student UX designer to redesign the whole queueing experience at the math study center of University of Washington.

Getting help is not easy.

The paper sheet. Tutors sometimes skip names on the list because of illegible handwritings or the noise during rush hours. Some students frequently walk up to the paper sheet to make sure they are not skipped.

The long wait. During rush hours, students might need to wait more than one hour to get helps and solve their homework problems. Collaboration is highly encouraged by tutors, but many of them are working alone while waiting.

Paperless Line-up

At University of Washington, homework of intro level calculus classes are online assignments. Therefore, students at the study center use laptop for math studying, which means the paper waiting list could be replaced by a web-based queuing system.

As line-up goes paperless, students no longer have to walk up and sign on a paper sheet. Keeping track of where they are on the line is just one click away. Also for tutors, names on computer screen are always legible.

With a deeper understanding of the story behind, we came up with a mechanism which changed the orginal way of getting someone to work with. It also motivates students to work with others. For students who come later, they can skip part of the long line by joining someone who is already in line.

credit to my teammate Annie Zhou

We presented the redesign on World Usability Day 2014 at University of Washington. Fascinated by the topic of our presentation, many UW students stopped by and provided helpful feedbacks on our design. Many of them suggested being passively matched by the system would not significantly make working together easier. It's a rare case that one can find two persons getting stuck on one exact same homework problem around the same time. Moreover, many students thought that if given the chance, they are open to just work with someone on the same class, in spite of whichever specific homework problem. Therefore, we refined the design to address this issue, designing for a more autonomous experience.

Be better informed.

A more autonomous experience requires more provided information.

The scrollable list on the left should provide enough information of each group in line so that students can decide if they want to work together with one of those groups. In order to make it easy for students to pick out their preferred groups they from the list, we deliberately worked on the list interface, ensuring that the most essentail info is most discoverable.

I coded an interactive prototype with HTML/CSS and jQuery, and tested it with four students. The overall feedbacks were positive, but still there were issues like not sure what info are required to submit, having difficulty finding the click to join, etc..
