QueuePool: a New Way to Line Up

The Math Study Center at University of Washington is a place for students to study on intro calculus classes. Tutors are there to help students with homework problems. Given that tutors are occupied most of the time, students who need help are lined up on a paper sheet to wait for their names to be called. QueuePool is the redesign that makes waiting easier and more productive.

Overview

I worked with another student UX designer to redesign the whole queueing experience at the math study center of University of Washington.

Getting help is not easy.

The paper sheet. Tutors sometimes skip names on the list because of illegible handwritings or the noise during rush hours. Some students frequently walk up to the paper sheet to make sure they are not skipped.

Working alone, waiting alone. Most of the times, students only work with friends they knew. Although collaboration is highly encouraged, still many of them prefer working alone rather than interrupting others.

Paperless Line-up

At University of Washington, homework of intro level calculus classes are online assignments. Therefore, students at the study center use laptop for math studying, which means the paper waiting list could be replaced by a web-based queuing system.

As line-up goes paperless, students no longer have to walk up and sign on a paper sheet. Keeping track of where they are on the line is just one click away. Also for tutors, names on computer screen are always legible.

Wait Productively.

Students with the same question are grouped by the system, so that they can work together while waiting in line.

We presented the redesign on World Usability Day 2014 at University of Washington. Fascinated by the topic of our presentation, many UW students stopped by and provided helpful feedbacks on our design. Most feedbacks were around the level of control being too little. They suggested being passively grouped with others by the system would not significantly make working together easier. It's a rare case that one can find two persons getting stuck on an exact same homework problem around the same time. Therefore, we refined the design to address the issue, advocating for a more well-informed and self-directed experience.
